Learning content
What is an MSI and why does it matter?

A musculoskeletal injury (MSI) is damage to muscles, tendons, ligaments, joints or nerves caused by work. In residential care, the most common MSIs are low back strains, shoulder injuries, and neck strain from repeated or awkward movement.

  • MSIs are the leading cause of lost-time injuries among BC care workers.
  • Manual resident handling is the highest-risk activity in residential care.
  • Risk is not only about weight — posture, angles, repetition and environment all matter.
Most MSIs are preventable with the right assessment, equipment, and technique.

The pre-task risk check

Before any resident handling task, SafeCare BC recommends a pre-task risk check. It takes less than a minute. Tap each area to see what to look for.

What is their mobility level today? Have there been any changes since the last shift? Are they cooperative or anxious?
Is the room clear enough to work in? Is the equipment in place and functioning? Is there a clear path for the lift or sling?
Is this the right time for this task? Do we have enough people? Is the equipment correct for this resident?
Am I physically and mentally ready for this task? Is there anything about my condition today that might affect how I perform it safely?
If any of those four checks raises a concern, you do not proceed without addressing it first.

Lesson 4 of 5
At Cedar Wing: The Habits Get Tested
Before repositioning, always

Four steps that take less than a minute and protect your back every time. Tap each one to read why it matters.

Adjust the bed to hip height
Adjust the bed so it sits at hip level when you are standing. This keeps your spine in a neutral position throughout the task. Working below hip height forces a bend that multiplies the load on your lower back with every movement.
Lower the rail on your side
Lower the bed rail on your working side before you touch the resident or the slide sheet. Reaching over a raised rail while leaning forward is the most common single cause of lower back MSI in repositioning tasks. Priya did not lower the rail. That was the moment.
Keep the load close to your body
The further the load is from your centre of gravity, the more force your spine absorbs. Step close to the bed. Do not reach across. If you cannot get close enough, reposition yourself before you reposition the resident.
Keep your spine neutral throughout
Keep your back in its natural curve throughout the task. If you need to go lower, bend from the knees, not the waist. A neutral spine distributes the load across the whole body. A bent or twisted spine concentrates it at the weakest point.
Key takeaway
Keep your spine neutral. Bend from the knees if you need to lower your height.
