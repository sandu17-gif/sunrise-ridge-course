Lesson 1 of 5
Before you begin

You are fourteen months into the job at Sunrise Ridge. You know the wings, the residents, and the rhythms of a shift. Today is different...

Priya is back!!

She has been off for six weeks with a lower back strain from a repositioning task. Experienced, careful, does not cut corners. She got hurt anyway.

Linda has asked you to be her buddy for the morning. Not to supervise, just to be a second set of hands and a familiar face.

What happened to Priya is not unusual. Musculoskeletal injuries are the leading cause of lost time in BC residential care.

Lesson 2 of 5
She's back!

You walk into the staff room, and Priya looks up from her mug.

Tap to read

"Hey. You are my buddy today, apparently. Stiff in the mornings but better by ten. Cleared for regular tasks with a second person. Nothing solo until week three. Linda will go over the rest." She says it matter-of-factly. Not looking for sympathy.

Injuries can happen to the careful ones too. Musculoskeletal injuries (MSI) are the leading cause of lost time for care workers in BC.

Lesson 3 of 5
Meeting Mrs. Okonwo

She arrived yesterday. Here is what you need to know.

Priya mentions she has not used a ceiling track lift since her training two years ago. She knows the steps — she just wants to take it slow. You will go through it together before you start.

You and Priya are about to enter Mrs. Okonwo's room for the first time. You run through the pre-task risk check mentally.

You knock and step in slowly. Mrs. Okonwo looks at you and then at Priya.

Tap to observe the room

You and Priya confirm the sling fit together, talking through each step with Mrs. Okonwo before moving. Eleven minutes later she is in the chair by the window, a pillow supporting her left side.

Lesson 4 of 5
At Cedar Wing: The Habits Get Tested

You head to Cedar Wing for Mr. Kowalski's morning repositioning. His handling plan indicates a two-person assist for all bed repositioning, using the slide sheet and a repositioning belt. No lift required if he can partially assist.

Priya pauses in the hallway, one hand on the wall. A twinge. It comes and goes, she says. You wait.

Later in the day...

You are in the lounge with coffee when Jonah comes over and sits down.

Priya puts down her mug and listens. Jonah had been helping with a transfer earlier that morning. The other aide said they could skip the stand aid. The resident was small, cooperative. It would be faster. "The handling plan said two-person assist with the stand aid," Jonah said. "I knew that. I just went along with it."

A critical moment:

Mrs. Okonwo has reported pain during the lift. You are mid-transfer. She is suspended above the chair. Priya has stopped the remote.

Do you lower her back to the chair immediately?
